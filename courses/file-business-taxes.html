How to File Business Taxes

Finance 7 lessons

Navigate the complexities of filing taxes as a music business owner. Stay compliant and maximize deductions.

Module 1

Why Filing Business Taxes Matters

9 min

Introduction to Business Taxes for Artists

Filing taxes as a business or self-employed artist is essential for staying legal and building credibility. The IRS requires all income to be reported — failing to file can result in fines and penalties.

As an independent artist, you're considered self-employed. This means you're responsible for both the employer and employee portions of Social Security and Medicare taxes.

Filing taxes as a business establishes legitimacy, which is useful for loans, grants, and future business opportunities.
Module 2

Tax Forms for Artists

10 min

Tax Forms for Filing as an Artist

Form 1040 is the main form for filing federal income taxes. As a self-employed artist, you'll also file Schedule C to report your business income and expenses.

If you worked as a freelancer or independent contractor, you'll receive a 1099-NEC form from clients who paid you more than $600. All income reported on 1099s must be included in your tax filings.

Many states also require self-employed individuals to file state tax returns. Check your state's requirements to determine the forms you need.

Understanding these forms is the first step to confident tax filing.
Module 3

Preparing for Tax Season

11 min

How to Prepare for Tax Filing

Keep detailed records of your income and expenses year-round:
• Income: Royalties, gig payments, freelance income
• Expenses: Equipment, marketing, travel, studio rentals

Use accounting tools like QuickBooks or Wave to track your income and expenses throughout the year. Consider tax preparation software like TurboTax, H&R Block, or TaxAct.

Maintain a system for organizing receipts and invoices, either digitally or physically. Be prepared to provide records of all business-related expenses to back up your tax deductions.
Module 4

Deductible Expenses for Artists

12 min

Deductible Expenses for Artists

Common deductions for artists include:
• Equipment: Instruments, recording gear, and software
• Travel: Airfare, hotels, and meals when performing or recording
• Marketing: Social media ads, website hosting, promotional materials

If you use part of your home exclusively for your music business, you may qualify for the Home Office Deduction. This allows you to deduct a portion of rent, utilities, and maintenance.

You can also deduct vehicle expenses — mileage or actual costs of using your vehicle for business-related travel.
Module 5

Filing Your Taxes

10 min

Filing Your Taxes

Step-by-step guide to filing business taxes:

1. Gather all income and expense records from the past year
2. Complete Schedule C and any other relevant forms
3. File online using tax software or hire an accountant

Online filing is ideal for simple returns and is typically cheaper. An accountant is better for complex returns or if you need personalized advice.

April 15th is the deadline for filing federal taxes. Late filing penalties are 5% of unpaid taxes per month, with interest accruing daily.
Module 6

Tax Tips and Strategies

8 min

Tax Tips and Strategies

Pay estimated taxes quarterly if you expect to owe more than $1,000 in taxes for the year. This avoids penalties and makes tax season less painful.

Work with a tax professional who understands the music industry. They can identify deductions you might miss and help you plan strategically.

Keep personal and business finances completely separate. Use a dedicated business bank account and credit card to make tracking expenses much easier.
Module 7

Staying Compliant Year-Round

7 min

Course Conclusion

Filing business taxes as an artist isn't just about compliance — it's about building a professional foundation for your career.

Key takeaways:
• Track everything year-round, not just at tax time
• Take advantage of every deduction you qualify for
• Consider professional help for complex situations
• Stay organized and file on time

Annual filing milestones: Q1 (April 15), Q2 (June 15), Q3 (September 15), Q4 (January 15 of next year).
